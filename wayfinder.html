Wayfinder: A Financial Wellness Tool

Wayfinder is a financial wellness tool offered by Great-West Life that helps Canadians plan and save for retirement. Launched as a pilot web service, the platform allows members to combine their workplace retirement plans and savings to see how they can improve their financial outcomes.


Launch website

Research

Our goal was for members to plan their financial goals, act on financial advice to achieve their goals faster, and sustain their financial wellness through Wayfinder. Finance is a very daunting subject that affects us every day and our intent was to make it more fun and easy to plan for something like retirement. Our design team worked with UX researchers and found three key learnings from our research:

Users have no time for financial planning. Financial planning wasn’t a huge priority for them, especially if they have kids or are already retired. With life happening, they don’t have time to shop around for rates or develop comprehensive financial plans.

Advice comes from everywhere. Some users find value in peer advice, others gather information from family or newspapers, and some put all of their faith in a financial advisor and are willing to pay for their services.

Retirement comes last. Immediate financial burdens take precedence over long-term saving. Some users felt that they couldn’t think about saving for retirement until their debts are gone. That said, many passively save by contributing to their bank investments but continue to keep their eye on smaller goals such as purchasing a house or paying for an education.

Wireframing the experience

Based on our research and inspiration from other banks and fintech platforms, we emphasized our design research on three key flows: onboarding (helping a member plan their financial goals in an effortless, timely manner), navigating the platform (helping the member understand their financial wellness at an overview level while getting more details at a goal-specific level), and maintenance (allowing a member to aggregate bank investments through a third party service; getting advice from a certified financial advisor through a third party service; using recommendation cards as a way to suggest financial advice for specific goals and accounts).


User testing our hypotheses

We tested our wireframes using Google’s HEART framework with a pool of 20 users that were similar to the personas our UX researchers came to us with. Our overall goal was to determine whether users felt comfortable with using the product and to validate that our key flows were quick, seamless and easy to understand. We tasked our users with registering for an account and setting up their retirement plan, in addition to determining and correcting a financial shortfall in one of their goals.

Our results came back with positive feedback: users found it very easy to navigate through the platform and some minor miscommunications only required design and copy tweaking. Many of our users found the platform helpful, but also felt skeptical about aggregating accounts because of platform credibility and security.

Design decisions

After our first round of user testing, we looked at different ways we could approach our design and colour palette. As an extension of Great-West Life, we kept it in-line with their brand colour, green, and introduced grays and blues. Our goal was to keep the design light and uncluttered.

We wanted to make saving for retirement easier for the user to understand. We worked together with the client and their copywriter to help us understand retirement and break it down in a simpler way. This was very important to the onboarding experience and the way that users interact with recommendation cards because we wanted users to act with certainty and without worry.

People have other goals between now and retirement, but we still wanted to emphasize overall financial wellness. Life happens and we want to help people prepare for that speed bump. Using hierarchy in layout, typography and colour, we highlight the wealth curve as soon as the user lands on the dashboard, but we also give them an at-a-glance of their other goals. We avoided using red for alerts on the dashboard because we felt it was intrusive and might negatively convey a critical problem with their finances. Instead, we opted for yellow, in contrast to green, to let users know that they have a shortfall that they need to correct.


Challenges

Accessibility. We found out later in the design process/early in development that the client wanted to meet WCAG Level AA standards. We had to redefine our colour palette because a lot of our design elements weren’t meeting the contrast ratios, especially our recommendation cards. This led to deadline pushbacks due to file adjustment and sign-off on new colour palettes.

User testing. Upon further research and experience, I discovered that there were better tools that assess how a user interacts with wireframes (we used InVision, which validated flows instead of the elaborate interactions). The way we set up the test also felt too prescribed, having added colour to our wireframes (many of our users thought the wireframes were high-fidelity designs).


Takeaway

Wayfinder was the first project I led from start to finish as a senior lead designer, working with several members of our design team, as well as our Creative Director. I learned some of my most valuable lessons as a designer with this project and this became a catalyst for my hunger for better experience and interface design. Some key takeaways:

Accessibility isn’t always ugly. We have a responsibility as designers to aim for inclusion, not WCAG levels. Accessibility isn’t always hard and isn’t always ugly, especially if we think about accessibility at the beginning of our projects and continue to keep it under consideration as we go through different phases of the project. I led a presentation about the importance of accessibility, with an emphasis on design best practices—our best practices align with accessible design, so we shouldn’t have to think of it as a separate checkbox.

We need 100% team transparency. We are experts at design, but just because the user experience and interface is beautiful doesn’t always mean it’s possible or right. Working around business requirements, deadlines and technical restraints happens all the time with projects; our weekly check-ins and open discussions on Slack allowed us to transparently communicate what could be done within our resources and finding common ground.

Dive deeper. We put our best foot forward with our designs. Although we anticipated more rounds of user testing, we were unable to test our designs before launch due to resource restraints. There were still many questions that needed to be answered and many decisions that needed to be validated. If there was an opportunity to maintain the product, I’d like to do further research and testing to find more ways to make the platform more efficient for the member to use, and how we could make the product more sustainable and usable so that the user thinks to use Wayfinder when they think of their financial wellness.
